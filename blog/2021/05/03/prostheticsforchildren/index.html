Prosthetics for Children

By Brandon Pentz | May 3, 2021

Prosthetics have been documented as early as the 1500s and have since then been vastly improved and redesigned as technology has advanced.(1) However, despite the vast advancements in prosthetic engineering, there still remains significant rejection rates within prosthetics. While determining the frequency and causation for rejection of a prosthetic can be complicated, several attempts have been made to understand the frequency and reasons. In one of the largest reviews on upper-limb prosthetic rejection rates, it was found that on average, 1 in 5 prosthetic users reject their upper-limb prosthetic (2). Common reasons for rejection in traditional prosthetics, such as myoelectric devices, tend to be related to comfort, heaviness, awkwardness (non-anthropometrical), and lack of functionality(3). These problems can be further exacerbated within child populations, largely a result of their high rates of growth(4) Further problems also arise not only from tangible issues related with comfortability and weight, but also with psychosocial issues. Thus, the need to address these common problems within prosthetics remains.

Aside from rejection rates within prosthetics, one of the primary issues is cost and accessibility. Especially with children, who require constant updates to their prosthetic devices, insurance coverage does not always cover the full cost for the device, let alone additional devices and upgrades (4). Thus, there has been a greater reliance on non-profit organizations to assist with updates in prosthetic devices (5). In Alberta, according to the Alberta Orthotic & Prosthetic Centre, all alberta citizens requiring a prosthetic device receive some coverage through the Alberta Aids to Daily Living (AADL), which requires a 25 percent deductible up until $500, whereby full coverage is in effect for the year (6). While there are some exclusions, such as highly expensive prosthetics such as the myoelectric hand, other coverage can be sought through private insurance companies, The War Amps of Alberta, Workers Compensation Board of Alberta, Veterans Affairs Canada, and Health Canada depending on the eligibility of the client. However, this coverage is not always homogenous globally and many developing countries seek foreign aid to receive prosthetic devices. While hopeful, there still remains many challenges with providing foreign aid with traditional prosthetics in developing countries, including limited customizability, lack of maintenance as users outgrow or require updates to their prosthetics, methods of distribution, and non-functional. (7) Notably, the delivery of highly technical prosthetic devices, such as myoelectrics, may not be as highly effective and impactful as mechanical, body-powered prosthetics.

Ultimately, the use of 3D-printed hand prosthetics has the potential to address some of these issues(8). Through 3D printing, the customizability of the prosthetic design is endless.Along with the selection of various materials, the prosthetic devices can be relatively lightweight, anthropometrically-designed, and cost-effective for both the supplier and client. Furthermore, 3D printing allows for effective distribution (i.e. accessibility) to virtually anywhere in the world with a 3D printer. Thus, the need for qualified prosthetists is not required and consistent updates in prosthetic materials are relatively seamless. Thus, the AICP Foundation plans to utilize 3D printing technology to address these common problems described above, through a collaborative effort, to innovate in child prosthetics and further advocate for their prosthetic needs.

About us

AICP Foundation serves to innovate and advocate for children requiring prosthetics. We plan to collaborate through a multi-disciplinary framework to design and develop 3D printed prosthetics specifically targeted towards improving impact and quality of life.
